Example 2.2.12.

The asteroid is the parametric curve \(\gamma(t) = (\cos^3 t, \sin^3 t)\) for \(t \in [0,2\pi]\text{,}\) shown in FigureÂ 2.2.13. I want to calculate its arclength.
\begin{align*} L \amp = \int_a^b \sqrt{ \left( \frac{dx}{dt} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{dy}{dt} \right)^2 } dt\\ \amp = \int_0^{2\pi} \sqrt{ (-3 \cos^2 t (\sin t))^2 + (3 \sin^2 t (\cos t))^2} dt\\ \amp = \int_0^{2\pi} \sqrt{ 9 \cos^4 t \sin^2 t + 9 \sin^4 t \cos^2 t} dt\\ \amp = \int_0^{2\pi} |3 \sin t \cos t | \sqrt{ \sin^2 + \cos^2 t} dt\\ \amp = \int_0^{2\pi} |3 \sin t \cos t | dt \end{align*}
The absolute value here causes trouble. A convenient way to drop it is to notice that both \(\sin t\) and \(\cos t\) are positive on \([0, \pi/2]\text{.}\) That range covers a quarter of the asteroid and the asteroid is symmetric, so I can calculate the length of that quarter and multiply by 4.
\begin{equation*} L = 4\int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} 3 \sin t \cos t dt = 6 \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \sin 2t = -3\cos 2t \Big|_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} = 6 \end{equation*}
