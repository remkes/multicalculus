Example 3.1.6.

I’ll start with at the domains of the five explicitly stated functions in the Example Example 3.1.4.
  • \(f_1\) and \(f_4\) are polynomials (in two variables), so there are no restrictions. Their domain is \(\RR^2\text{.}\)
  • \(f_2\) is a sine function, which again imposes no restrictions, so it also has domain \(\RR^2\text{.}\)
  • \(f_5\) has two square roots, one involving \(x\) and one involving \(y\text{.}\) Therefore, \(x\geq 0\) and \(y \geq 0\) are necessary to define \(f_5\text{.}\) That domain is the positive \(x\) and \(y\) quadrant, including the origin and the positive pieces of both axes.
  • \(f_6\) also has a square root. To ensure that the square root has a positive argument, I require \(4 - x^2 - y^2 \geq 0\text{,}\) or \(x^2 + y^2 \leq 4\text{.}\) This domain is a a circular disc of radius \(2\text{,}\) including its boundary.
  • Lastly, \(f_3\text{,}\) has an strange exponential. This leads to very strange domain behaviour. If \(y\) is an integer, \(x\) can be any non-zero real number. If \(y\) is a fraction, \(x\) must be positive if the denominator of \(y\) is even, to avoid square roots of negative numbers. If \(y\) is irrational, \(x\) must be positive. This all leads to a very complicated domain in \(\RR^2\text{,}\) something hard to describe directly as a shape.
