The \(\RR^2\) case is useful to understand the general situation. In this case, \(x\) and \(y\) are in input (domain) and \(z\) is the output (range). I can think of the graph as a height function: over some point \((x,y)\) in the domain of \(f\text{,}\) the graph sits at some height \(z = f(x,y)\text{.}\)
